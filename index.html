MY STORY AT AGE 13

How Math Unlocked My Dream of Making Games

When I was 13 years old sitting in a math classroom, I loved video games and desperately wanted to program them myself. But I ran into a major obstacle: Computers don't inherently understand what space is.

They don't inherently understand walls, character rotation, or weapon ranges. Every time I wanted to figure out if Pac-Man hit a boundary, or how an Asteroids ship turned, I discovered that the answers were sitting right on the dry-erase board in math class.

MAP ARCHITECTURE

1. Handling Boundaries: Grids vs. Pixels

When I started, I tried reading actual pixel colors to see if a player hit a wall. But if the game lagged or the character moved too fast, it would glitch completely through.

The math solution is a Functional Grid System. We map the game world into a 2D matrix array where every grid cell coordinate (x, y) outputs a specific value: 0 for empty paths, and 1 for solid walls.

2. Coordinate Transformations

In vector arcade games like Asteroids, ships and obstacles are shapes defined by geometric point vertices on a grid coordinate graph layout.

To manipulate objects smoothly, the engine uses core rules: Translation modifies coordinates to move the ship, Rotation spins it around an origin pivot point, and Reflection instantly flips scale parameters across an axis line.

3. Vector Splitting & Parabolas

When aiming a defense turret or firing a missile, a player chooses an angle and speed. How does a computer use that information to change positions?

We use trigonometric ratios to split speed into component forces: horizontal speed becomes Vx = Speed × cos(θ), and vertical speed is Vy = Speed × sin(θ). Gravity then subtracts values from Vy linearly, turning a straight launch vector into a quadratic parabola trajectory.

4. Distance Tracking & Collisions

How does a game engine calculate if a player is close enough to collect a coin, hit an enemy, or trigger an explosion? It relies completely on the Pythagorean Theorem.

By measuring horizontal difference (Δx) and vertical difference (Δy) between the center point vertices of both entities, we compute a² + b² = c². If the length of the hypotenuse (c) is less than or equal to the sum of both circle radii, a solid intersection registers!
